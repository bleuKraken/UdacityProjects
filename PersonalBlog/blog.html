The Birth of Virtual Reality as an Art Form

Author: John Smith

Chris Milk is a director, photographer, and known by many as a innovator and immersive artist. Accompanied by Joshua Roman on cello and McKenzie Stubbert on piano, in this Ted Talk given on February 2016 Milk discusses how virtual reality is the next medium to share stories with others. He begins his story from the first moment he remembers putting on headphones to his current work with creating breakthrough virtual reality projects. VR is the last medium for storytelling, he says, because it closes the gap between audience and storyteller.

The Power of Music

Chris Milk shares that when he was a kid, he experienced something so powerful, he spent the rest of his life searching for it. What he experienced wasn't virtual reality; it was music. Music goes straight to the emotional vein, into your bloodstream, and right into your heart. It deepens every experience. Music gives everything a more emotional resonance. The right piece of music at the right time fuses with us on a cellular level.

Milk began directing music videos to try and recreate the feelings that he experienced from music. Adding moving pictures over the music added narrative dimension, but it never quite equated the power of raw music on its own. He began to wonder what would happen if he was able to involve the audience.

Virtual Reality as A New Medium

Along with Aaron Koblin, Milk started auditioning new technologies that could bring people inside the work. They were pushing beyond the screen, trying to connect more deeply to people's hearts and imaginations.

With VR there is a complete surrounding environment where a participant may be able to turn around, look up and down, and maybe even walk around.

Thanks to the technologies of spatialized audio, the replication of sounds that an object or source make from a distance in an enviornment, the participant can be locked into the experience because even if you turn your head to left or right, even behind you, the sound will rotate accordingly. It can also be used to direct your attention to where you should be looking as a story or experience unfolds.
